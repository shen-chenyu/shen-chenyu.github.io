(Physics Note) Oscillator and Wave

2020-04-28 0 min read

A useful way to understand waves is to stop treating them as a new kind of object. A wave is what a collection of coupled oscillators looks like after the correct collective coordinates have been chosen. The harmonic oscillator is the local unit; the wave is the diagonalized motion of many such units.

1. The single oscillator

The harmonic oscillator is the minimal model of motion near a stable equilibrium. If \(q(t)\) is the displacement from equilibrium, the Lagrangian is

\[ L=\frac{1}{2}m\dot q^2-\frac{1}{2}kq^2 . \]

The Euler--Lagrange equation gives

\[ m\ddot q+kq=0, \qquad \omega_0=\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} . \]

The solution is a rotation in phase space, \(q(t)=A\cos(\omega_0t+\phi)\). The important point is not the sinusoidal form itself, but the fact that a quadratic energy produces a linear equation, and a linear equation can be decomposed into independent modes.

2. Coupled oscillators

A wave appears when many oscillators are coupled. Consider a one-dimensional chain with displacement \(u_n(t)\), mass \(m\), lattice spacing \(a\), and nearest-neighbor spring constant \(K\). A simple Lagrangian is

\[ L=\sum_n\left[ \frac{1}{2}m\dot u_n^2 -\frac{1}{2}K(u_{n+1}-u_n)^2 \right]. \]

The equation of motion is

\[ m\ddot u_n=K(u_{n+1}-2u_n+u_{n-1}). \]

Translation symmetry suggests the normal-mode ansatz

\[ u_n(t)=A e^{i(kna-\omega t)} . \]

Substitution gives the dispersion relation

\[ \omega(k)=2\sqrt{\frac{K}{m}}\,\left|\sin\frac{ka}{2}\right| . \]

Each value of \(k\) labels an independent oscillator. The many body problem has not disappeared; it has been diagonalized. The wave is the language in which the coupled system becomes a collection of independent harmonic motions.

3. The continuum limit

At long wavelength, \(ka\ll 1\), the dispersion becomes

\[ \omega(k)\simeq c|k|, \qquad c=a\sqrt{\frac{K}{m}} . \]

In this limit the lattice displacement \(u_n(t)\) is replaced by a smooth field \(u(x,t)\), and

\[ u_{n+1}-2u_n+u_{n-1}\approx a^2\partial_x^2u . \]

The equation of motion becomes the wave equation

\[ \partial_t^2u-c^2\partial_x^2u=0 . \]

The continuum wave equation is therefore not an isolated postulate. It is the long-wavelength effective equation of a coupled oscillator system. The field \(u(x,t)\) is the coarse variable, and \(c\) is the emergent speed of information propagation through the chain.

4. Field action and energy

The continuum wave equation follows from the action

\[ S[u]=\int dt\,dx\, \left[ \frac{\rho}{2}(\partial_tu)^2 -\frac{T}{2}(\partial_xu)^2 \right], \qquad c=\sqrt{\frac{T}{\rho}} . \]

Here \( ho\) is a mass density and \(T\) is an elastic stiffness or tension. The corresponding energy density is

\[ \mathcal E= \frac{\rho}{2}(\partial_tu)^2 +\frac{T}{2}(\partial_xu)^2 . \]

The first term is kinetic energy. The second is elastic energy stored in gradients. This is the field version of the oscillator energy \(p^2/2m+kq^2/2\): time variation and spatial variation exchange energy as the wave propagates.

5. Boundary conditions and normal modes

On a finite interval \(0

\[ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0, \]

the normal modes are

\[ u_n(x,t)=A_n\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\cos(\omega_nt+\phi_n), \qquad \omega_n=c\frac{n\pi}{L} . \]

The continuous field has become a countable collection of oscillators. This is the same idea in reverse: many coupled local degrees of freedom become independent collective modes.

6. Physical picture

The conceptual chain is

\[ \text{stable equilibrium} \rightarrow \text{harmonic oscillator} \rightarrow \text{coupled oscillators} \rightarrow \text{normal modes} \rightarrow \text{waves}. \]

The oscillator is local. The wave is collective. The normal mode is the coordinate system in which the collective motion becomes simple. In this sense, waves are not fundamentally more mysterious than oscillators; they are oscillators seen through the correct symmetry-adapted variables.
