Designing Workflows for Unfinished Questions

2026-06-20 15 min read

Thesis: AI-assisted research workflows should not be judged by how convincing their explanations sound. They should be judged by whether unfinished questions become precise enough to meet resistance.

Research question: can a language-model workflow turn an unresolved research tension into a non-obvious next action better than a direct prompt or a simple typed checklist?

Design: compare simple and structured routes, then re-read their outputs through support checks, label checks, baseline comparisons, and bounded local checks.

Result: the useful output was a sharper pause-and-reset question, not evidence that the larger workflow architecture should be trusted. The more portable contribution is a discipline for stopping before fluent interpretation outruns contact.

A note on status: this workflow is retired, not paused. Before running it I fixed a go/no-go condition in advance — the structured route had to produce action-changing frames that a simple baseline could not. It did not clear that bar, so I stopped extending it rather than rebuild the reporting around a weaker result. What survives is the discipline for making that call, not the architecture.

A surprisingly common failure mode in research tooling is that the system becomes better at describing a problem than at changing the research state. The language gets sharper. The diagrams get cleaner. The summaries become easier to read. But the next experiment, calculation, or counterexample remains basically unchanged.

This note is about a method for resisting that failure. The method starts from a simple idea: an unfinished question is valuable only if it has causal force over future work. It should change what is remembered, what is tested, what is ignored, and when a line of thought is stopped.

A question is alive when it changes the next action. If it only improves the wording of the story, it is not yet a research object.

1. Treat unfinishedness as a resource

In actual research, progress often begins with something that refuses to close: a measurement that does not fit the story, a limiting case that behaves differently than expected, a toy model that explains too much, or a proposed mechanism that cannot survive a simple control.

The temptation is to resolve the tension quickly. An AI-assisted workflow makes that temptation stronger, because a language model can usually produce a smooth explanation for an incomplete situation. But smoothness is dangerous. A polished paragraph can erase the very friction that made the problem interesting.

A better workflow preserves unfinishedness in a form that can be acted on. It does not merely say, "this is a deep tension." It asks: what observation would split the alternatives? What minimal model would make the ambiguity visible? What result would make us change the next step?

Here is what that looked like in practice, because the idea is easier to feel than to define. The workflow was not one model answering a prompt. It was a small cast of roles taking turns on the same unfinished tension, each trying to do something the others could not — closer to a study group arguing than to an oracle explaining.

  • The problem-former tries to name the real question hiding inside a vague unease.
  • The toy-builder builds the smallest model that would actually show the effect.
  • The counterexample-hunter tries to break that model before anyone gets attached to it.
  • The boringness-critic keeps asking the rude question: is this just the obvious answer in fancier words?
  • The reconstructor keeps only what survived the beating and hands it back, sharper.

Two pieces of vocabulary make the rest of this readable. A frame is action-changing when it changes the next experiment, not merely the wording of the story. And resistance is shorthand for any contact — a control, a calculation, a toy that can fail — that can make a proposed frame change, split, or die. The whole game is to push a vague tension until it meets something that can push back.

A concrete run looked like this. Hand the cast a system that seems to "remember" its history, and the interesting move is not a long essay on memory; it is the toy-builder and counterexample-hunter together forcing the question into a fork the world could answer — gradual erasure consistent with a slowly relaxing internal variable, or a plateau suggesting a structure that cannot be reset on that timescale. Section 6 walks that fork out in full. The point here is the texture: the value came from the roles refusing to let a smooth story stand.

2. Interest is behavior, not psychology

I do not think an isolated model has human scientific interest. Human interest is embodied, historical, emotional, and social. It involves taste, embarrassment, persistence, risk, and a kind of personal ownership that current language models do not have.

But a system can still implement a weaker and more useful substitute. A notebook, planning routine, code runner, review routine, and memory store can be arranged so that unresolved questions keep returning and keep shaping the next move. In that sense, "interest" becomes a property of the workflow:

an unresolved question has interest-like force when it repeatedly changes attention, memory, tests, and stopping decisions.

This distinction matters. The goal is not to make an AI sound curious. The goal is to make a research process less willing to close a question before it has encountered something that can push back.

3. Physics metaphors were tools, not claims

A lot of the design intuition came from physics, and this was the part that was the most fun to build. It is tempting — and genuinely useful — to picture the space of half-formed research frames as a physical system: a landscape with valleys where a story has settled, ridges that are hard to cross, frustration where competing constraints cannot all be satisfied at once, and phase changes where a small push flips the whole picture.

The most ambitious version pushed this all the way. Treat each candidate frame as a state. Let frames that pull in different directions be coupled, like spins in an Ising model, so that a configuration carries an "energy" — how much unresolved tension it holds. Then wander that space with Monte Carlo or annealing moves: accept a jump that lowers the energy, and occasionally accept an uphill one to climb out of a shallow, tidy trap. For a while the workflow really was a little statistical-mechanics sandbox for ideas, with branches coupled in a mesh and a running log of accepted and rejected moves.

The binding test, though, was operational. A physics-inspired concept had to name a variable, a control, a falsifying test, or a state update before it could touch a decision. "Energy" was not allowed to mean "how much I like this idea"; "frustration" was not allowed to mean "several agents disagree." If a metaphor cleared that bar it shaped the workflow; if it did not, it stayed a design intuition rather than a decision variable. That was not a betrayal of the imagination work — it was the next honest test.

So the Ising graphs, the energy and frustration scores, the Monte Carlo and annealing search, the mesh between branches were excellent thinking aids — they shaped how I approached the problem, and they were a pleasure to build. As mechanisms, though, each owed an answer to one question: what does it measure, which move does it accept or reject, and which simpler baseline does it have to beat? On that question they remained diagnostic — useful for intuition and logging, never graduated to decision variables, and deliberately kept switched off until something simpler could first show value.

The clearest survivor was the humblest one: coarse-graining. It helped name a pause-and-reset fork between temporary kinetic memory and a more structural residue. That was a good question, not a finished result. Everything more elaborate stayed paused.

4. What I actually tested

I treated the workflow as a stress test rather than a product demo. The input was not a blank request for ideas; it was an already unfinished research situation with an ambiguity that needed to become a next action.

The concrete case was a memory-like response problem: a system seemed to depend on its recent history, but it was unclear whether the relevant state was a gradually relaxing kinetic variable or a more persistent structural change. I used that case as a demanding testbed, not as a claim about the system itself.

The comparison had three practical reference points: a direct prompt, a typed checklist, and a richer staged workflow that preserved alternatives, asked for a bounded check, and forced a revision after either an observation or a clear blocker. The question was whether the richer process changed the action, not whether it produced a more impressive essay.

The experiment therefore had three layers. The first layer generated candidate problem frames under comparable budgets. The second layer audited whether those frames were supported, testable, and not merely shaped by prompt labels. The third layer asked whether a candidate could travel all the way from a proposed frame to a bounded action, a check or a precise reason the check could not run, an observation, and an updated next move.

Operationally, this made the experiment less like a contest between writing styles and more like a state-transition test: different routes could generate a candidate frame, but every route had to pass through the same audit boundary and then become either a bounded check, a precise blocker, or a changed next move.

In practice this meant several repeated constructions, not one impressive run: direct prompting, typed baselines, richer staged workflows, checks for labels biasing the output, revisiting saved outputs, and finally pushing the strongest local frame toward a discriminating test. The purpose was to learn where structure actually changes research motion, and where it only makes the same motion easier to narrate.

The stress test had to be falsifiable in three ordinary ways. First, a richer workflow had to differ from a plain request or a careful checklist. Second, the difference had to survive checks for labels steering the answer. Third, the result had to move beyond a frame into a bounded action, a check or a precise blocker, and a changed next move. Failing any one of those tests meant the system had made a better artifact, not necessarily a better research state.

Looking across the whole project, the most important pattern was not which construction looked most elaborate. It was that each evidential layer narrowed the claim. Calibration made the filters less gullible. Replaying saved outputs showed how many attractive frames still lacked an executable center. Local contact checks showed that a frame can sharpen a question without justifying the larger mechanism that produced it.

Two numbers were especially clarifying. In a broad replay of saved candidates, only six of 122 cases reached a bounded local check; many stopped because they lacked executable parameters or a real control. In the final local comparison, six of 14 cases reached that kind of bounded check and the negative control was rejected, but the candidates converged around the same pause-and-reset test shape and simple typed baselines remained strong. These are not statistical success rates; they are audit counts from pressure tests. Their value was to sharpen one question without licensing a larger claim.

The most telling moment of the experiment was also its most deflating, and it is worth saying plainly: this was a null result. A direct typed checklist asking for a single discriminating observation reached the same pause-and-reset fork as the richer multi-agent route. When a simpler construction lands in the same place, the elaborate one has to justify itself by doing something the simple one cannot — and here it did not.

The scope was narrow in a way worth stating directly: the live comparison rested on a single demanding seed, not multiple independent domains, and nothing in it checked whether the frames actually changed what a researcher did next. The counts above are pressure signals from that one case — enough to pull method lessons from, not enough to claim the workflow forms better questions in general, or that the hypothesis itself is viable.

The most useful result was negative in a productive sense. A lot of added structure improved bookkeeping. It helped record why an idea looked plausible, but it did not automatically make the next test better. Several different workflows also converged toward the same obvious test shape, which made their architectural differences less meaningful than they first appeared.

The richer process mattered only when it forced a discriminating contrast: one observation that would separate two explanations, or one missing contact point that made further interpretation dishonest.

5. Make ideas meet resistance

By "resistance" I mean any contact that can make a proposed frame change, split, or die. It can be a calculation, a simulation, a counterexample, a limiting case, a measurement, or even a precise statement that the required evidence is not available.

The important point is that resistance must be stronger than a preference for good prose. A claim has not advanced merely because it has been restated in a cleaner vocabulary. It advances when some bounded action can put pressure on it.

A useful pattern is: 

unfinished tension
  -> problem frame
  -> next bounded action
  -> check or clear blocker
  -> observation
  -> changed next move

The "clear blocker" case is important. Sometimes the right outcome is not a result, but an honest statement that the required data, instrument, infrastructure, or experimental condition is missing. That is still better than pretending a local check, a preference score, or a beautiful explanation is enough.

6. A small example of a better question

Consider a system that seems to remember its history. One explanation is kinetic: the memory is carried by a slowly relaxing internal variable. Another explanation is structural: the intervention has changed something more persistent in the system itself.

A weak workflow might produce a long discussion of "memory," "hysteresis," or "hidden state." A stronger workflow asks for an intervention that separates the explanations. For example: if we vary the duration of a pause and the duration of a reset, do we see gradual erasure consistent with kinetic relaxation, or a plateau suggesting that the relevant structure cannot be reset on that timescale?

The value of that question is not that it sounds sophisticated. The value is that it names a possible split in the world. It says what kind of observation would matter. It turns a vague mechanism into a testable contrast.

This was the best kind of local signal the exercise produced: not proof, but a better fork. It changed the next action from "discuss memory" to "measure whether pause length encodes a continuous relaxation variable, and whether reset length erases the response or leaves a plateau." That is a real improvement in problem formation. It is still not external evidence.

7. Respect the evidence boundary

The same example also shows where discipline is needed. A local toy check can be useful. It can sharpen the question and reveal whether a proposed intervention is even coherent. But it is not the same as external contact with data or an independent measurement process.

This is where many AI-assisted workflows are tempted to keep going. They can add another review layer, another score, another report, another synthetic example. But if the missing piece is real contact, the honest move is to stop at the boundary rather than convert interpretation into evidence.

The limitations matter. This was not a broad scientific benchmark, and it did not include independent domain-expert review or new external measurements. It used saved outputs, local deterministic checks, and one especially informative memory-like case. That is enough to extract method lessons, but not enough to claim that the workflow produces better science.

In the strongest local case, the workflow did produce something I would keep: a pause-and-reset fork that separated gradual kinetic erasure from persistent structural residue. That is exactly the kind of question-forming improvement I was looking for. But the remaining needed contact was outside the local artifact. Without data, a real measurement setup, or an independent measurement process, the right conclusion was: good question, insufficient evidence.

8. Design rules I would keep

The strongest lessons from building this kind of workflow were not about which prompt was cleverest. They were about the traps that make a system appear research-like without improving the research state.

The first lesson is that structure is necessary but not sufficient. A system can fill fields, produce clean diagrams, and name a plausible test while still failing to create a stronger next move. Structure helps only when it forces the question to specify a variable, a control, a possible observation, and a way the frame could be revised.

The second lesson is that review criteria should be treated as brakes, not as rewards. They can catch vague protocols, unsupported claims, missing controls, and decorative metaphors. But passing those checks does not mean the idea has scientific value. It only means the artifact is less likely to be misleading.

A related lesson is that calibration and generation are different achievements. A filter can correctly reject fluent foils and still say nothing about whether the workflow can produce a non-obvious next action. The generator has to be tested separately, preferably by replaying saved outputs under stricter rules than the ones that produced them.

The third lesson is that simple baselines are more important than they feel. If a careful checklist, a direct prompt, or a plain typed note produces the same intervention, then the elaborate workflow has not earned its complexity. The burden is on the richer process to create a different next action, a sharper split, or a better stopping condition.

The fourth lesson is that labels can contaminate interpretation. A name borrowed from physics, statistics, or systems theory can make an ordinary idea look deeper than it is. The test is whether the label does operational work: does it add a variable, a constraint, a control, or a failure mode?

The fifth lesson is that a useful local check should be respected but contained. It can sharpen a question and reveal whether an intervention makes sense. It should not be promoted into external evidence unless it has actually made contact with the relevant data, system, or measurement process.

The sixth lesson is that apparent workflow diversity can collapse into the same attractor. If direct prompts, checklists, and richer workflows all produce the same test shape, the lesson is not that the elaborate workflow has proved itself. The lesson is that the problem itself may be pulling everyone toward an obvious next move, or that the workflow labels are steering the outputs.

The seventh lesson is that figures should be treated as arguments, not decoration. A useful schematic removes unnecessary detail until the structure of the decision is visible: alternatives, checks, evidence boundary, and possible next action.

The eighth lesson is that stopping can be the most important output of the workflow. A missing external contact point is not an invitation to add another synthetic review layer. It is information about what the idea still lacks.

Rule Why it matters
Preserve unresolved residue. Do not let summaries erase the part of the problem that still does not make sense.
Demand a discriminating action. A next step should separate alternatives, not merely gather more agreeable context.
Treat evaluation as a brake. Review criteria can catch weak artifacts, but they should not become evidence of scientific value.
Separate calibration from evidence. A filter passing hand-built examples means it is less gullible, not that the generator forms better questions.
Replay saved outputs under stricter rules. Re-reading old artifacts often reveals missing controls, missing parameters, or shape convergence.
Compare against simple baselines. If a complex workflow only reproduces what a simple prompt or typed checklist would do, the complexity is not justified.
Stop at missing contact. When the next necessary step is data, measurement, or an experiment, do not replace it with another report.
Treat blockers as evidence about the workflow. A precise blocker can be useful, but repeated blockers mean the method is no longer testing the idea.
Watch for shape convergence. Different workflows that produce the same obvious next test have not shown distinct problem-forming power.
Make diagrams earn their place. A figure should clarify the decision structure, not merely decorate the argument.

9. What AI can still be good for

None of this makes AI useless for research. Quite the opposite: language models can be very helpful at turning a vague tension into candidate frames, surfacing controls, proposing toy models, generating alternative explanations, and checking whether a planned test actually distinguishes the alternatives.

But that usefulness is highest when the workflow is designed around resistance. The model should not be asked only to explain. It should be asked to help expose what would make the explanation fail, what would make two mechanisms diverge, and what missing evidence prevents a stronger claim.

The practical ambition is modest but important: build tools that protect good unfinished questions from being prematurely closed by fluent language.

The contribution I would keep is a criterion, not a finished system: preserve unresolvedness only when it can be translated into a variable, a contrast, a bounded action, a possible observation, and a reason to stop. Without that chain, the workflow is only improving the story around the question.

10. The discipline of stopping

The most valuable part of a research workflow may be its ability to say: this is interesting, but not enough. The question is sharper than before, but the evidence has not crossed the boundary. The next honest step is not another layer of interpretation, but a stronger form of contact.

That stopping discipline is not pessimism. It is how an unfinished question stays useful. It remains alive not because it is protected from failure, but because it is kept close to the conditions under which it could fail.
